Before You Begin

The hospital is not in charge of you.

You are walking into a medical environment staffed by professionals who genuinely want a healthy outcome for you and your baby. They are your allies, not your adversaries. But the hospital is also a system — with routines, protocols, rotating staff, and time pressures that don't always center your specific wishes and values.

This module teaches you how to navigate that system as a team. How to communicate clearly, ask the right questions, advocate without creating conflict, and make decisions together under pressure. Not to fight the hospital — but to be informed, active participants in everything that happens inside it.

The partner's role in this module is specific and critical: you are the communicator, the question-asker, and the steady presence that allows the birthing person to focus entirely on labor while someone they trust handles the logistics.

Part 1 — Your Rights

Informed consent is your legal right.

Before any procedure during labor, your care provider is ethically and legally required to explain what they want to do, why, what the risks and benefits are, and what your alternatives are — and then give you the opportunity to say yes or no. This is called informed consent.

Here is what the research actually shows about how consistently this happens:

```
44.7%
of oxytocin administrations occurred without consent being sought
Jacques et al., IJGO, 2025 · 7,394 women
60.2%
of episiotomies performed without consent being sought

<20%

Häggsgård et al., PLoS ONE, 2024 · 2,800 women

These numbers are not from developing countries with limited resources. They are from France and Sweden — two of the most developed healthcare systems in the world. The gap between what informed consent is supposed to look like and what actually happens during labor is significant.

"What matters most for a positive birth experience is not whether everything goes perfectly — but whether the birthing person felt involved in the decisions being made about their body and their baby."

Hodnett, American Journal of Obstetrics and Gynecology, 2002 · confirmed by VIP Study, Hinton et al., 2023

The same research confirms the inverse: when decisions are made for people rather than with them — even medically appropriate decisions — those births are significantly more likely to be experienced as traumatic, disempowering, and distressing. The intervention itself matters less than whether the person felt heard and included.

One finding of particular importance for partners: the 2025 French study found that women who had a birth preferences document were significantly less likely to report that consent was missing. Having a document — and a partner who knows how to use it — changes the dynamic in the room.

Part 2 — Your Decision Tool

The BRAIN framework — five questions that change everything

The BRAIN framework is one of the most widely taught tools in doula training, midwifery education, and childbirth preparation programs worldwide. DONA International — the world's largest doula certifying organization, with over 12,000 certified doulas across 56+ countries — teaches informed decision-making as a core competency, and the BRAIN framework is the primary tool used in DONA-approved training to teach this skill.

When your care provider recommends any intervention or procedure during labor, walk through these five questions:

```
B
Benefits
"What are the benefits of doing this right now?"
R
Risks
"What are the risks if we do this?"
A
Alternatives
"Are there alternative approaches we could try?"
I
Intuition
"What does our gut tell us?"
N
Nothing
"What happens if we do nothing or wait?"

An interventional study found that shared decision-making about labor interventions — the core principle the BRAIN framework encodes — significantly improved childbirth experience scores and satisfaction compared to routine care where providers made decisions for the mother. The framework works not because it delays care, but because it ensures both of you understand what you're agreeing to.

Important context: BRAIN is not a tool for refusing care or creating conflict. Most of the time, after asking these questions, you will understand the recommendation and agree with it. The questions are about being an informed participant — not an adversary. The goal is shared decision-making, not resistance.

The "N" — Nothing — is often the most powerful question. The doula and midwifery literature emphasizes that asking "what happens if we wait 20 minutes and reassess?" is one of the most useful questions in the birth room. Many interventions that feel urgent are actually time-sensitive but not immediately critical — and a brief pause to discuss can make the difference between a decision made under panic and one made with clarity.

Practice BRAIN scenarios together before labor. The more familiar the framework feels, the easier it is to use under pressure.

Part 3 — Communicating as a Team

Advocate without creating conflict

The partner's role in the hospital is not to be a gatekeeper or a guard. It is to be a communicator — calm, clear, curious rather than confrontational. The research is consistent: collaborative communication between couples and care providers produces better outcomes and higher satisfaction than adversarial dynamics.

```

Here are the real scenarios you will likely encounter and how to navigate them:

Scenario 1 — A new nurse comes on shift and doesn't know your preferences

Shift changes typically happen every 12 hours. Research from The Joint Commission identifies patient handoffs as one of the leading sources of communication breakdown in hospitals. Your new nurse received a clinical briefing — but likely not the personal context of your birth preferences.

"Hi, I'm Tony — this is Cassandra. We're so glad you're here. We have a birth preferences document — can I give you a copy? The main things that matter most to us are [X] and [Y]. Is there anything you need from us to make this work well?"

Warm, brief, specific. You are informing — not demanding. You are building rapport in the first 60 seconds, which shapes every interaction that follows.

Scenario 2 — A procedure is recommended and you need time to decide

Your care provider recommends augmentation, internal monitoring, or another intervention. You want to discuss it as a couple before agreeing.

"Thank you for explaining that. Can we have just two or three minutes to talk through this together before we decide? We want to make sure we're on the same page."

This is entirely reasonable in any non-emergency situation. In an emergency — when the care team communicates urgency — respond accordingly. But most labor decisions allow for a brief conversation. Claiming that time is not obstructive. It is your right.

Scenario 3 — The birthing person is overwhelmed and can't speak for themselves

During active labor and transition, the laboring person may not be able to communicate clearly with care providers. This is one of the most important moments for the partner to step in — calmly and respectfully.

"She's in the middle of a contraction — can we give her 30 seconds? She'd like to answer that herself when she's ready." Or: "We discussed this before — her preference is [X]. Can you help us with that?"

You are not speaking over her. You are protecting her space to be in labor while ensuring her preferences are represented. Know her wishes before you get to the hospital so you can speak with confidence.

Scenario 4 — An unexpected recommendation you weren't prepared for

The care team recommends a cesarean, a forceps delivery, or another intervention you hadn't anticipated.

"We want to understand this fully. Can you help us understand — what's driving this recommendation right now? What happens if we wait? What are our options?"

These are not combative questions. They are the questions any informed patient has the right to ask. In a genuine emergency your care team will communicate that urgency clearly — and when they do, trust them. In the absence of urgency, BRAIN is your tool.

Part 4 — Institutional Realities

Shift changes, rotating staff, and how to handle them

Research confirms that labor ward handoffs — the moments when one nurse or midwife hands care of you to another — are associated with communication gaps that can affect your experience. A study of patient handover perceptions found that families often felt uninformed and uncertain during these transitions.

Here is your shift change protocol — five steps that take 90 seconds and make a significant difference:

```
1
Introduce yourselves by name
"Hi — I'm Tony, this is Cassandra." First names immediately make the interaction personal rather than transactional.
2
Acknowledge the transition warmly
"We're really glad you're here — we know shifts are long." A moment of recognition builds goodwill immediately.
3
Offer your birth preferences document
"We have a birth preferences document — can I give you a copy so you have our priorities?" A physical document is more reliable than verbal handoff notes.
4
State your two or three most important preferences
"The things that matter most to us right now are [delayed cord clamping] and [skin-to-skin immediately after birth]." Keep it specific and short.
5
Open the door to collaboration
"Is there anything you need from us to make tonight go smoothly?" This signals you are a partner, not a problem.

Bring multiple printed copies of your birth preferences document to the hospital — one for the chart, one to hand to each new nurse or midwife. Research confirms that women with birth plans were significantly less likely to report missing consent for procedures. The document is not just a wish list — it is a communication tool that actively changes how your care is delivered.

```
Part 5 — Your Birth Preferences Document

Birth plans work — when held the right way

A systematic integrative review of 11 studies across six countries found that birth plans were associated with positive outcomes when developed collaboratively with care providers. Women with birth plans reported higher satisfaction, greater sense of control, and better obstetric outcomes. A randomized controlled trial confirmed significantly higher childbirth experience scores for women who had a birth plan versus those who did not.

Here is what the evidence and doula literature say about how to write and use one effectively:

```
Keep it to one page. Care providers are busy. A one-page document with clear bullet points gets read. A four-page document with every contingency mapped out gets set aside. Priority, not comprehensiveness.
Use preferences language, not demands language. "Our preference is delayed cord clamping" lands differently than "We insist on delayed cord clamping." The first opens a conversation; the second starts a negotiation.
Discuss it with your provider at a prenatal appointment before labor. The 2023 scoping review found that couples who discussed their birth plan with providers ahead of time were better prepared for decision-making under stress. The conversation the plan generates is as valuable as the plan itself.
Include a flexibility statement. Something like: "We understand birth is unpredictable. These are our preferences, not demands. We trust your expertise and want to work together." This signals you are partners, not adversaries.
Know the difference between preferences and non-negotiables. Some things matter to you deeply; others are preferences you hold loosely. Know which is which before you arrive — so when circumstances change, you can adjust with clarity rather than grief.
Bring more copies than you think you need. At minimum: one for the admission chart, one for each nurse (you may have 2-3 during a long labor), one for yourself to reference.

Module 4 will walk you through building your birth preferences document together — step by step. You will leave that module with a completed, personalized document ready to print and bring to the hospital.
