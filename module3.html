How the Hospital System Works

The hospital is not designed to be confusing. But if you've never been a patient in one during labor, it can feel that way. Understanding who does what — and how the system actually functions — changes how effectively you can advocate.

The Reframe

You are not a visitor. You are a participant.

Most non-birthing partners arrive at the hospital feeling like they're entering someone else's territory — a clinical environment where the staff are in charge and you are along for the ride. This framing is both common and counterproductive.

The hospital is where your baby will be born. The staff are providing a service. You and your partner have rights — to information, to time to make decisions, to ask questions, to decline procedures, and to expect respectful communication. Understanding the system doesn't make you difficult. It makes you prepared.

```
28%
of birthing parents report feeling pressured to accept procedures they didn't fully understand during labor. Knowing the system and having a prepared partner significantly reduces this rate. Declercq et al., Listening to Mothers III, 2013.
```
Who's in the Room

Who does what — and who to talk to when

```
Labor & Delivery Nurse
Your primary point of contact throughout labor. The L&D nurse monitors your partner and baby continuously, manages medications, responds to changes, and communicates with the OB or midwife. They spend more time with you than any other provider.
→ Go to your nurse first for questions about what you're seeing, what the monitor shows, how labor is progressing, and what to expect next. Build this relationship early — introduce yourself by name.
OB / Attending Physician
The physician responsible for your partner's care. In many hospitals, the OB is not continuously present — they check in periodically and are called for significant developments, pushing, and delivery. Your partner may not have met this specific doctor before.
→ When the OB is in the room, it matters. Ask your most important questions then. If you haven't met them, introduce yourself. They make the clinical decisions.
Midwife
In a midwife-led model (hospital, birth center, or home), the midwife provides continuous or near-continuous care throughout labor. Midwives typically have a different communication style — more collaborative, more explanation, more time. The same advocacy principles apply.
→ In a midwife-led birth, you have more direct access to the primary decision-maker. Use it. Ask questions freely.
Anesthesiologist
Provides epidural analgesia if requested. Typically comes to the room specifically for that procedure, then is available on-call for adjustments. Has a brief but significant role.
→ If your partner wants an epidural, request it before active labor intensifies — there is often a wait. Ask the nurse when to make the request.
Resident / Medical Student
In teaching hospitals, residents and students may be involved in your care. You have the right to decline their participation. If you are comfortable with their presence, they can be a valuable resource for questions.
→ You are never required to have residents or students present. If you prefer not, simply tell the nurse. No explanation required.
Doula (if applicable)
A trained birth support professional hired separately from the hospital. Your doula provides continuous non-clinical support and can be a valuable translator between the clinical team and your family. Distinct from nursing — cannot make clinical decisions.
→ If you have a doula, coordinate with them before labor. Establish how you'll work together as a team. Your roles are complementary, not competitive.
```
Arriving at the Hospital

The first 30 minutes — what happens and what to do

The admission process happens during a period when your partner is already in labor. It is procedural and fast-moving, and it sets the tone for everything that follows. Here's what to expect and how to navigate it well.

```
Triage assessment
Being evaluated before admission

When you arrive, your partner will likely be assessed in triage to determine whether they're in active labor and ready for admission. This involves a cervical check, monitor placement, and clinical assessment. You may or may not be allowed in the triage space depending on the facility.

If you're separated during triage: stay calm, stay near the entrance, and don't interpret the separation as a problem. It is standard procedure at many facilities.

If asked to wait: Use the time to prepare — have the birth preferences document accessible, know the parking situation, eat something if you haven't.
First contact with your nurse
Set the tone early

When you meet your labor and delivery nurse, introduce yourself by name. Hand them a copy of your birth preferences document. Say something like: "We have a birth preferences document — we'd love a few minutes when things settle to walk through any questions together."

This signals that you are informed, collaborative, and not adversarial. It invites partnership rather than establishing tension. The nurses who know you as a person — not just a family member — will communicate with you differently.

Print three copies of your birth preferences. One for the chart, one for the nurse, one for shift changes. Label them clearly.
The monitor and the IV
What gets attached and why

Most hospital admissions involve continuous fetal monitoring (two belts on the abdomen — one for contractions, one for fetal heart rate) and an IV access port (not necessarily a running IV — just a port for quick access if needed). Both are standard.

If continuous monitoring is not in your birth preferences and you are low-risk, you can ask about intermittent monitoring options. This is a reasonable request. Use BRAIN if there is a clinical reason being given for continuous monitoring.

```
Rights You Have — and May Not Know About

Informed consent is not a formality. It is a right.

Every medical procedure performed during labor requires informed consent — meaning your partner must be told what is being proposed, why, what the risks are, and what the alternatives are before agreeing. This is not a bureaucratic requirement. It is a legal and ethical standard.

You have the right to ask for time to discuss a decision. You have the right to ask for a second opinion. You have the right to decline a procedure. Exercising these rights does not make you a difficult patient — it makes you an informed one.

```
The partner's role in informed consent

The decision belongs to the birthing parent — not to you. Your role is to make sure they have the information they need to decide, and that they're not agreeing to something out of exhaustion, confusion, or pressure. Ask the questions. Give them the space to answer. Then support whatever they choose.

```

Lesson 2 — The BRAIN Framework in Real Time
Lesson 2 of 4

The BRAIN Framework in Real Time

BRAIN is the most practical informed consent tool available to laboring families. This lesson teaches you how to use it in real clinical situations — not just what the letters stand for.

The Framework

Five questions. Every intervention.

When your care provider recommends any procedure or change in plan, BRAIN gives you a structure for gathering the information you need before agreeing. It is not a tool for refusing care. It is a tool for understanding care — and for making sure your partner's agreement is genuinely informed.

```
B
Benefits
Ask now
"What are the benefits of doing this right now?"
What specific improvement or outcome is the care team expecting? Why is now the right time? What problem does this solve?
R
Risks
Ask now
"What are the risks if we do this?"
What are the possible downsides, side effects, or complications? How common are they? Are there risks to the baby, the birthing parent, or both?
A
Alternatives
Ask now
"Are there alternative approaches we could try first?"
Is there a less invasive option? Could we try a position change, more time, or a different approach before this intervention?
I
Intuition
Check in
"How does this feel to us?"
How does the birthing parent feel about this recommendation? Does something feel off, or does the reasoning make sense? Intuition is valid data.
N
Nothing
Most powerful
"What happens if we wait or do nothing right now?"
Can we take 15–20 minutes to discuss? What changes if we don't act immediately? Many decisions that feel urgent allow for a brief pause.
```
Using BRAIN in Real Scenarios

What it sounds like in an actual conversation

```
Scenario 1
The nurse recommends Pitocin augmentation

The nurse says labor has slowed and the OB is recommending Pitocin to strengthen contractions.

BRAIN in practice:

"Can you help us understand — what specifically has slowed, and what's the benefit of starting Pitocin now vs. waiting?" (Benefits, Nothing)

"What are the risks of Pitocin for her and the baby, and are there things we could try first — like a position change or walking?" (Risks, Alternatives)

Then check in with your partner privately: "How does this feel to you?" (Intuition)

Note: After asking BRAIN questions, most families understand and agree with the recommendation. The goal is informed consent — not obstruction. If the clinical reason is clear and the risks are acceptable, agreeing is the right outcome.
Scenario 2
The OB recommends a cesarean for failure to progress

After several hours at the same dilation, the OB recommends a cesarean section.

Is this an emergency? First — ask. "Is this an emergency situation or do we have time to ask a few questions?" Most failure to progress situations are not immediate emergencies.

BRAIN questions: "Can you tell us specifically what has and hasn't changed over the last few hours?" (Benefits — understanding the clinical picture)

"What are the risks of the cesarean, and what are the risks of continuing to wait?" (Risks)

"Is there anything we could try — a position change, more time, breaking the waters if they're intact — before surgery?" (Alternatives)

Your role here: Be calm, be clear, and be collaborative. You are gathering information, not fighting. If the care team has a strong clinical reason, listen to it. If something doesn't feel right, say so respectfully.
Scenario 3
A procedure is proposed without full explanation

A staff member arrives to perform a procedure without fully explaining what it is.

It is always appropriate to ask: "Can you tell us what you're about to do and why?" This is not obstructive. It is a standard, reasonable, patient-centered question. Any clinical staff member should be able to answer it.

You are never required to agree to a procedure you don't understand. "Can we have a moment?" is a complete sentence.
```
The Critical Exception

When BRAIN does not apply — genuine emergencies

```
⚠️
In a genuine emergency, respond immediately. When your care team communicates clear and urgent necessity — "we need to act right now" — trust them and act. BRAIN is for non-emergency decisions. A true emergency looks and sounds different from a recommendation. You will know the difference because the care team will communicate it clearly and with urgency. If you are unsure whether it is an emergency, ask: "Is this urgent or do we have time to ask a few questions?" The answer will tell you everything.

The vast majority of decisions in labor are not emergencies. Most feel urgent because of the clinical environment and the emotional stakes — but most allow for a brief pause and a few questions. The BRAIN framework makes that pause productive rather than just anxious.

```

Shift Changes — Protecting Your Birth Preferences

A long labor will cross at least one nursing shift change — possibly two or three. Each time, your care team changes and your birth preferences need to be re-established. This lesson teaches you exactly how to do that.

Why This Matters

Shift changes are the most common point where preferences get lost.

Hospital nursing shifts typically run 12 hours — 7am to 7pm and 7pm to 7am. A labor that starts at noon and ends at 6am crosses two shift changes. Each new nurse receives a clinical handoff report but may not have read your birth preferences document in detail — or at all.

The incoming nurse doesn't know your names. Doesn't know what the previous nurse already discussed with you. Doesn't know that you have a birth preferences document, what's in it, or what conversations have happened. Starting fresh each shift is normal. Your job is to make that reset happen quickly and well.

```
12h
standard nursing shift length in most US hospital labor and delivery units. A labor averaging 12–18 hours for first-time parents will cross at least one shift change — often at the most critical phase of labor.
```
The Shift Change Protocol

A five-step process for every new nurse

```
1
Introduce yourself by name
The first thing you do when the new nurse walks in. Stand up, make eye contact, extend your hand if appropriate.
"Hi, I'm [name] — [partner's name]'s partner. Thank you for being here."
2
Hand them a copy of the birth preferences
Have a fresh copy ready before the shift change happens. Don't wait to be asked.
"We have a birth preferences document — here's a copy for you."
3
Give a brief, honest status update
One or two sentences on where things are emotionally and physically. Not a complaint — context.
"She's been in active labor since about 2pm. She's managing well / having a hard time right now. The last few hours have been [calm / intense / difficult]."
4
Identify one or two most important preferences
Don't recite the entire document. Highlight what matters most right now in the context of current labor status.
"The two things most important to us right now are [delayed cord clamping] and [being asked before any new procedure is started]. Everything else is in the document."
5
Invite them in as a partner
End with a collaborative invitation rather than a set of demands. This changes the dynamic entirely.
"We're really glad you're here. Is there anything you need from us to get oriented quickly?"
```
Timing the Handoff

When shift changes happen — and when they don't

Standard shift change happens at 7am and 7pm. However, if your partner is actively pushing or birth is imminent, the outgoing nurse will typically stay until delivery rather than hand off mid-birth. This is standard practice and you can ask the outgoing nurse directly: "Will you be here for the birth?"

If a shift change happens during active pushing or transition, it is appropriate to ask — calmly — whether the outgoing nurse can stay for continuity. Most will if they are able. If they cannot, the five-step protocol above still applies — just compressed into 60 seconds.

```
Anticipate, don't react. If you know a shift change is coming — it's 6:30pm and you've been in labor since noon — prepare your preferences copy and your introduction before the new nurse walks in. Being ready to hand it over immediately signals competence and care.
```
When the Same Preference Keeps Getting Ignored

Escalating respectfully — and effectively

Occasionally a preference is acknowledged but not followed. If the same preference is repeatedly overlooked across staff interactions, there is a respectful escalation path.

Step 1: Restate the preference directly and calmly to the staff member. "I want to make sure we're on the same page — we'd like to be asked before any new procedure is started."

Step 2: If it continues, ask to speak with the charge nurse — the supervising nurse for the unit. "We'd like a few minutes with the charge nurse when that's possible."

Step 3: If still unresolved, ask to speak with the patient advocate. Most hospitals have a patient advocate or patient relations team available 24 hours.

These steps are rarely needed. Most preferences are respected when communicated clearly and collaboratively. But knowing the path exists — and using it calmly when needed — is part of being a prepared partner.

Lesson 4 — When Things Don't Go to Plan
Lesson 4 of 4

When Things Don't Go to Plan

Birth rarely follows a script. This lesson is not about preparing for disaster — it's about being the steady presence your partner needs when the plan changes, so neither of you is caught off guard.

The Reality

Most births include at least one significant deviation from the plan.

Birth preferences are not guarantees — they are expressions of values and intentions. Labor is unpredictable. The plan you arrive with may need to change because of clinical developments, because your partner changes their mind, or because labor simply goes differently than anyone expected.

This is not failure. This is birth. And how you respond to the pivot determines your partner's experience of it more than the pivot itself.

```

"Women who felt supported and informed during unexpected changes in their birth plans reported significantly higher satisfaction with the birth experience than those who felt blindsided — regardless of the outcome."

Hodnett et al., 2002 · Cochrane Review · Caregiver support for women during childbirth
```
Common Pivots — What to Expect and How to Respond ```
Pivot 1
The planned unmedicated birth becomes an epidural birth

This is the most common plan change in labor. Many people who plan unmedicated births request an epidural — and this is a completely valid decision made in real circumstances that no amount of preparation could fully anticipate.

Your role: Zero disappointment. Zero "but you said you wanted to go natural." Whatever you privately feel about the change, keep it to yourself. What you say in that moment matters more than you know.

What to say: "Absolutely. Whatever you need. I'm with you." Then move into the epidural-birth support mode from Module 2.

The most damaging thing a partner can do at this moment: Express hesitation, ask "are you sure?", or say anything that implies the change is a failure. The decision belongs entirely to the birthing parent. Your job is unconditional support.
Pivot 2
Labor stalls and augmentation is recommended

After hours of active labor, dilation stops progressing. The care team recommends Pitocin to augment contractions.

Use BRAIN. Ask about the specific clinical picture. Understand the risks and alternatives. Give your partner the information they need to decide. Then support whatever they decide.

Emotionally: A stalled labor is demoralizing. Your partner may feel like their body is failing. Your role is to reframe it: "Your body has been working incredibly hard. This is a tool to help it finish the job." Do not express your own frustration or impatience.

Pivot 3
An unplanned cesarean is recommended

Whether for failure to progress, fetal heart rate concerns, or another clinical reason, an unplanned cesarean is one of the most emotionally difficult pivots in birth.

If there is time: Use BRAIN. Understand the clinical reason. Ask about the urgency and the alternatives. Your partner needs to consent with full information, not just compliance.

If it is an emergency: Move immediately. Your support role in the OR includes holding your partner's hand, staying by their head, narrating calmly what you can see ("they're getting started," "everything looks calm in here"), and being the first face they see when the baby is born.

Emotionally: Your partner may feel grief about the birth they planned. This is real and valid. Do not rush them to feel better. Do not say "at least the baby is healthy" as a response to grief. Hold space for the complexity of it.

Your grief matters too. Partners are often invisible in the emotional processing of birth complications. If you are struggling, find a moment — later, not now — to process it. With your partner, with a trusted person, or with a professional if needed.
Pivot 4
Your partner changes their mind about something mid-labor

They said they didn't want visitors — now they want their mother. They said they wanted music — now they want silence. They said they'd never want an epidural — now they're asking for one.

People are allowed to change their minds. Labor is not a contract. What someone says they want at 32 weeks is not binding at 8 centimeters. The birthing parent's wishes in real time take absolute precedence over any prior stated preference — including preferences stated to you.

Your only job when plans change: Adapt immediately, without comment, without "but you said," without hesitation. The plan serves the person. When the person changes, the plan changes.
```
Staying Grounded

How to manage your own response to unexpected change

When the plan changes, you will feel something — anxiety, disappointment, fear, helplessness. These are normal responses to an unpredictable situation with high emotional stakes. The question is not whether you'll feel them — it's whether you'll process them in a way that serves your partner or burdens them.

During the birth: Hold your response. Your partner's experience is the priority. Process later.

After the birth: Give yourself permission to process whatever you felt. Talk about it with your partner when you're both ready. Talk about it with someone you trust. The partner's emotional experience of birth is real and often unacknowledged — it deserves space.

```
The thing that doesn't change

Whatever happens — however far the birth departs from the plan — your job remains exactly the same: make sure your partner never feels alone in it. That job is achievable in every possible birth scenario. It doesn't require the plan to hold. It requires you to stay present.
