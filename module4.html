What Birth Preferences Actually Are

The birth plan has a reputation — some care providers dread them, some parents over-invest in them. Both responses miss what a well-built birth preferences document actually does.

The Misunderstanding

A birth plan is not a plan. It's a communication tool.

The word "plan" sets up the wrong expectation. It implies that labor follows a script — that if you plan correctly, birth will unfold accordingly. It doesn't. Birth is the most unpredictable significant event most people will experience. The value of a birth preferences document has nothing to do with controlling outcomes.

What it does instead: it communicates your values, your priorities, and your informed preferences to care providers who have never met you. It starts a conversation. It establishes you as informed, collaborative partners — not passive patients. That communication, not the document itself, is what matters.

```
❌ What it isn't
A legally binding contract that care providers must follow regardless of clinical circumstances.
✓ What it is
A communication of your values and informed preferences that guides decision-making when choices arise.
❌ What it isn't
A guarantee of a specific birth experience or protection against medical interventions.
✓ What it is
A way to ensure care providers know what matters to you before decisions need to be made quickly.
❌ What it isn't
A list of demands that positions you as adversarial to your care team.
✓ What it is
An invitation to partnership — telling your care team what's important so they can support you more specifically.
❌ What it isn't
Something to be written at 38 weeks after a quick Google search for templates.
✓ What it is
The product of conversations between both partners about values, research about options, and discussion with your care provider.
```
The Research Case

Why it matters — what the evidence says

```
59%
of birthing parents who did not have a birth preferences document reported feeling that their preferences were not considered during labor. Among those who did have one and discussed it with their provider, that number dropped significantly. Lothian, Journal of Perinatal Education, 2006.

The benefit is not that the document forces compliance. The benefit is that it creates a conversation before labor — when everyone is calm, when there is time to discuss, when both partners can engage as informed participants rather than reactive decision-makers under pressure.

"Birth plans that reflect informed decision-making and collaborative communication — rather than a list of demands — are associated with higher satisfaction regardless of whether the birth followed the plan."

Pennell et al., 2011 · Midwifery · Birth plans and satisfaction with the birth experience
```
The Partner's Specific Role

Why this document needs both of you

Most birth preferences documents are written by the birthing parent alone — sometimes without the partner's input, sometimes without the partner even reading it. This is a missed opportunity and a liability.

The partner is the person most likely to need to reference the document during labor. They are the one who will hand it to the nurse during a shift change. They are the one who will use it as the basis for BRAIN conversations when a decision needs to be made. If the partner didn't help write it, they don't own it — and a document you don't own is one you won't use confidently.

The process of creating the document together — discussing each section, learning about each option, deciding what matters to you as a couple — is arguably more valuable than the document itself. The conversation is the preparation.

```
The key constraint

One page. Maximum two. A birth preferences document that requires more than 90 seconds to read will not be read during a busy labor and delivery shift. Every preference you include should be genuinely important to you. Preferences you include because you've seen them on other people's templates — but don't actually care about — dilute the ones you do care about. Less is more.

```

Building Yours — Evidence-Based Decisions

This lesson walks through the major categories of birth preferences — what the evidence says about each option, what the decision actually involves, and what to consider before choosing.

How to Use This Lesson

Not a checklist. A decision framework.

Each section below covers a category of choices that commonly appear in birth preferences. For each one you'll find what the options are, what the evidence says, and what questions to discuss together. After this lesson you'll use the template in Lesson 4 to record your actual choices.

Work through this together — both partners. For each category ask: What matters to us here, and why? The answer to that question is your preference. The research tells you what's evidence-based. Your values tell you what's right for you.

Labor Environment

Setting the stage — what you can control

```
Lighting, sound, and stimulation
Low-cost, high-impact · Discuss with your care team at admission

The hospital environment is clinically functional — bright, loud, and stimulating by default. Research on the stress response in labor is clear: lower stimulation supports relaxation, which supports oxytocin, which supports labor progress.

Preferences to consider: dimmed lights, door closed when possible, minimal unnecessary staff entries, personal music or silence, familiar scents. These are low-stakes requests with meaningful physiological impact.

Partner note: You are the environment manager. Your job at admission is to assess the room and make these adjustments — without asking every single time. Dim the lights when you arrive. Close the door. These don't require permission.
Freedom of movement and position
Evidence-based · ACOG supports mobility in low-risk labor

ACOG (American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists) guidelines support freedom of movement during labor for low-risk patients. Walking, position changes, use of a birth ball, and upright positions are associated with shorter labor and reduced pain perception.

Continuous fetal monitoring can limit mobility. If mobility matters to you, ask about wireless monitoring options — many hospitals now have telemetry monitors that allow walking while monitoring continues.

Who is present
Your call · No clinical default

You have complete say over who is present during labor and birth. Family members, doulas, friends — or just the two of you. There is no correct answer. The question is: who makes the birthing parent feel safest and most supported?

Consider: will additional support people be able to stay calm and follow your lead? Will they require management? A well-meaning family member who creates additional stress is not support. Be honest in your assessment.

```
Labor Management

The clinical decisions you may face — and what the evidence says

```
Intermittent vs. continuous fetal monitoring
Evidence: intermittent monitoring is appropriate for low-risk labor

Continuous electronic fetal monitoring is standard in most hospitals but is associated with increased intervention rates without improving outcomes for low-risk pregnancies. Intermittent auscultation (periodic checking with a handheld doppler) is endorsed by ACOG, AWHONN, and WHO for low-risk labor.

If mobility is important, ask about intermittent monitoring at your prenatal appointment. Clinical status may change this recommendation during labor.

IV access
Standard practice · Hep-lock vs. running IV

Most hospitals place an IV access port (hep-lock) at admission. This provides immediate IV access if needed without requiring a running IV line. The difference matters: a hep-lock allows full mobility. A running IV means a pole and tubing.

If you prefer mobility, a hep-lock rather than a running IV is a reasonable request for low-risk labor.

Eating and drinking during labor
Evidence evolving · ACOG updated guidance 2017

ACOG updated its guidance in 2017 to support light eating during low-risk labor, moving away from the previous nothing-by-mouth policy based on outdated anesthesia risks. Many hospitals still have restrictive policies. Ask your provider what their specific policy is and why.

At minimum, drinking clear fluids during labor is widely supported and important for endurance — especially in long labors.

Rupture of membranes
Decision point · Use BRAIN

Membranes (the amniotic sac) may rupture spontaneously or be ruptured artificially by the care team (AROM — artificial rupture of membranes). AROM is sometimes recommended to speed labor, but the evidence for its routine use is mixed and it typically initiates the clock on labor management decisions.

If AROM is recommended, this is a BRAIN conversation. Understand the specific clinical reason and what changes once membranes are ruptured.

```
Pain Management

All options are valid. Yours is the one that fits your values.

```
Epidural analgesia
Evidence: effective · Decision: entirely the birthing parent's

Epidural analgesia is highly effective for pain relief in labor. It does not increase cesarean rates when used appropriately. It may slow labor in some cases and can affect the pushing stage. Some people find that the trade of pain for loss of sensation and mobility is worth it; others don't.

There is no evidence-based reason to choose or avoid an epidural. It is a values decision. The partner's role: support whatever is chosen, without expressed preference or pressure.

Non-pharmacological pain management
Evidence: effective for coping · Covered in Module 2

Counter-pressure, position changes, hydrotherapy, breathing techniques, and continuous support all have evidence for reducing pain perception and improving coping in labor. These are not alternatives to epidural — they are tools that work regardless of pain management choice and before the epidural is placed.

```
Birth and Immediately After

The decisions around the moment of birth — what to consider

```
Delayed cord clamping
Evidence: beneficial · ACOG supports for 30–60 seconds minimum

Waiting 30 seconds to 3 minutes (or longer) before clamping the umbilical cord allows additional blood transfer from the placenta to the newborn, improving iron stores. ACOG supports delayed cord clamping for at least 30–60 seconds for all newborns. This is a low-stakes, high-benefit preference to include.

If the partner wants to cut the cord, note this in your preferences. Ask in advance — it is a simple and meaningful opportunity for many partners.

Skin-to-skin contact
Evidence: strongly supported · For birthing parent and partner

Immediate skin-to-skin contact between newborn and birthing parent (and partner, if desired) supports thermoregulation, breastfeeding initiation, bonding, and newborn physiological transition. The evidence is robust. Most hospitals support this as standard practice — but noting it in your preferences ensures it happens even during busy deliveries.

Newborn procedures
Know what's routine · Consent applies here too

Routine newborn procedures include eye ointment, vitamin K injection, and the hepatitis B vaccine. These are recommended by the AAP. Some families have preferences about timing, consent, or specific procedures. If you have preferences here, note them — and discuss them with your pediatrician before birth, not for the first time in the delivery room.
```

How to Present It to Your Care Team

A well-built document badly presented can still undermine its purpose. This lesson covers exactly how to introduce, share, and reinforce your birth preferences at every stage of care.

Before Labor — At Your Prenatal Appointment

The most important presentation happens before you're in labor.

The single highest-leverage moment to discuss your birth preferences is not in the labor room — it is at a prenatal appointment at 34–36 weeks, with your OB or midwife, when everyone is calm and there is time for an actual conversation.

Bring a draft. Walk through the sections that matter most. Ask: "Are there any preferences here that you'd have clinical concerns about? Are there things we've noted that wouldn't be possible at our birth location?"

This conversation does three things: it catches incompatibilities before labor, it signals to your provider that you are informed and collaborative, and it means your provider has seen your preferences before labor — not for the first time while managing a room full of activity.

```
Partner note: Both of you should be at this appointment if possible. You are both authors of the document. You both need to hear the provider's responses. You both need to own the final version.
```
At Admission

The first five minutes — set the tone immediately

```
1
Have copies ready before you walk in
Three printed copies in the hospital bag. Not in your phone. Not emailed. Printed, clean, easy to hand over. In the chaos of arrival, fumbling for a document on your phone signals the opposite of preparation.
2
Introduce it with collaborative framing
The tone of your introduction matters as much as the content.
"We have a birth preferences document we'd love to share with you when you have a moment. We know birth is unpredictable and we trust your expertise — we just want to make sure you know what's important to us."
3
Ask for acknowledgment — not agreement
You don't need the nurse to commit to every item. You need to know they've read it.
"Is there anything in there that you have questions about or that we should talk through?"
4
Keep one copy accessible to you at all times
Your copy is for you — for shift changes, for BRAIN conversations, for your own reference. Don't give away all three copies. Keep one in hand.
```
During Labor — Keeping It Relevant

How to reference it without making it adversarial

During active labor is not the time to pull out the document and read through it point by point. By the time you're in active labor, the document should already be in the chart and with your nurse. Your job is to reference specific preferences when relevant decisions arise — not to enforce the document comprehensively.

When a decision arises that touches a stated preference: say simply "We noted in our preferences that we'd like to discuss this before proceeding — can we take a moment?" Then use BRAIN.

What not to do: Wave the document at the care team during an active clinical moment. Reference a preference in a tone that implies the team is violating an agreement. Treat the document as a scorecard after the birth.

```
The tone that gets the best results

The families who get the best results from their birth preferences are not the ones who are most assertive. They are the ones who are most clearly informed, genuinely collaborative, and consistently calm. Care providers respond to partnership. The document is a tool for partnership — not a shield or a weapon.

```
When the Plan Changes

Holding preferences loosely — without letting go entirely

When something in the plan changes for clinical reasons, the right response is BRAIN — not the birth preferences document. The document expresses preferences. BRAIN gives you the process for making informed decisions when those preferences need to be reconsidered.

After a change: acknowledge it together. Note it for your own birth story. Don't process it publicly in the birth room — there's time for that later. The best thing a partner can say when the plan changes: "I've got you. We're doing this together."
