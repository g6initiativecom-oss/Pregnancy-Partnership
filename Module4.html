Before You Begin

This isn't a form. It's a conversation.

A birth preferences document is one of the most misunderstood tools in childbirth education. It is not a contract. It is not a guarantee. It is not a way to control your birth.

It is a conversation starter — with your care provider before labor, and with each other right now. The research is clear: the couples who benefit most from birth planning are not the ones who wrote the most detailed document. They are the ones who talked about it together, aligned their expectations, and walked into the birth room knowing each other's priorities.

This module gives you the evidence behind evidence-based preferences, teaches you how to hold your document with flexibility, and then walks you through completing your own Birth Preferences Template together — the one that goes in the hospital bag.

Part 1 — Why Do This Together

The process matters as much as the plan

```

"A more positive birth experience was associated with higher relationship satisfaction and fewer depression and anxiety symptoms for both parents at two months, 14 months, and two years postpartum."

Hein et al. · Journal of Affective Disorders · 2023 · Nearly 2,000 couples

A 2024 qualitative study from Radboud University Medical Center found that when couples collaborated on a birth plan, partners reported feeling more prepared and more actively involved during labor — and birthing parents reported gaining specific insight into their partner's expectations that they hadn't known before. The planning conversation revealed things they hadn't talked about. That conversation is the point.

A 2022 meta-analysis found that marital satisfaction declines significantly during the first year after the birth of a first child — but that couples who engaged in shared preparation experienced slower declines, particularly among fathers. Building your birth preferences document together is not just birth preparation. It is relationship preparation.

And there's one more finding worth sitting with: research shows that women who had birth plans that had not been discussed with their providers actually reported lower satisfaction and a lower sense of control than women who had no birth plan at all. The document alone isn't the intervention. The conversation is.

```
Part 2 — Evidence-Based Preferences

What the research says about specific preferences

These are the preferences most commonly included in birth plans — with the evidence behind each. Understanding the "why" means you can advocate for these preferences with confidence, and decide which ones genuinely matter to you.

```
Delayed Cord Clamping
Strong evidence

Waiting at least 30–60 seconds — and often 1–3 minutes or until the cord stops pulsing — before clamping and cutting the umbilical cord. Current ACOG guidelines support delayed cord clamping for all births as standard of care.

Why it matters: A Cochrane review of 48 trials involving over 5,000 term newborns found that delayed cord clamping was associated with significantly higher hemoglobin and ferritin levels at 3–6 months — meaning better iron stores. A 2022 RCT found that delayed clamping until cord pulsation stopped (average 5 minutes) significantly improved developmental scores at 2 years in preterm infants. For term births, the benefit is primarily iron status and early neurological development.

McDonald & Middleton, Cochrane, 2013; Andersson et al., 2022; ACOG Committee Opinion, 2020
Immediate Skin-to-Skin Contact
Strong evidence

Placing the baby directly on the birthing parent's (or partner's, in a cesarean) bare chest immediately after birth — before any non-urgent procedures — and keeping them there for at least the first hour.

Why it matters: A systematic review of 38 studies found that early skin-to-skin contact significantly increased exclusive breastfeeding rates and duration, reduced neonatal pain responses, and improved thermoregulation (temperature stability). Additional research shows it promotes maternal attachment, reduces postpartum hemorrhage through oxytocin release, and decreases infant crying and stress hormones. The first hour after birth — sometimes called the "golden hour" — is a critical window for bonding and breastfeeding initiation that, once missed, cannot be replicated.

Moore et al., Cochrane, 2016; Neczypor & Holley, Nursing for Women's Health, 2017
Freedom of Movement During Labor
Well-supported

The ability to walk, use a birth ball, change positions, and use hydrotherapy during labor — rather than being confined to bed with continuous monitoring unless medically indicated.

Why it matters: Lamaze International's Healthy Birth Practices, based on comprehensive evidence reviews, identify freedom of movement as one of the six core practices for healthy birth. Research shows that upright positions and movement during labor are associated with shorter first stages, less need for pain medication, fewer episiotomies, and higher satisfaction. ACOG's own guidance acknowledges that for low-risk labors, intermittent monitoring — rather than continuous electronic fetal monitoring — is appropriate and supports mobility.

Lamaze International Healthy Birth Practice #2; Lawrence et al., Cochrane, 2013; ACOG Practice Bulletin on Intrapartum Fetal Monitoring
Delayed Non-Urgent Newborn Procedures
Increasingly supported

Requesting that non-urgent newborn procedures — eye drops, vitamin K injection, first bath — be delayed until after the first hour of skin-to-skin contact, unless medically necessary to do sooner.

Why it matters: Eye prophylaxis (erythromycin drops) is legally required in most US states and cannot be waived, but it can typically be delayed 1 hour. Vitamin K injection prevents a rare but serious bleeding disorder — declining it carries real risk and couples should discuss this with their provider. The first bath is the most flexible: a 2022 systematic review of studies involving over 39,000 newborns found that delaying the first bath for at least 6 hours prevents hypothermia, helps maintain blood sugar, and significantly improves exclusive breastfeeding rates at discharge.

Gupta et al., Journal of Global Health, 2022; Neczypor & Holley, 2017
Early Breastfeeding Initiation
Strong evidence

Initiating breastfeeding within the first hour after birth — ideally during skin-to-skin contact — before supplementation, pacifiers, or formula unless medically indicated.

Why it matters: WHO and UNICEF recommend breastfeeding within the first hour as a "Baby-Friendly" standard. Research shows that early initiation significantly improves long-term breastfeeding duration and exclusivity. The colostrum present in the first feedings — sometimes called "liquid gold" — provides passive immunity, gut colonization with beneficial bacteria, and important developmental signals. The partner's role in protecting this window — by holding space, keeping the environment calm, and supporting positioning — is active and meaningful.

WHO/UNICEF Baby-Friendly Hospital Initiative; Moore et al., Cochrane, 2016
```
Part 3 — Holding It Loosely

When birth doesn't go as planned

```

"When birth deviates from the plan, trauma is prevented not by the plan being followed — but by the person feeling heard, informed, and included in decisions."

Bell et al. · Midwifery · 2022 · Systematic integrative review

The research on birth trauma and unmet expectations is clear: what determines whether a deviation from the birth plan becomes a source of distress is not whether the plan was followed — it is whether the person felt like an informed participant in what happened. A cesarean birth from a birth plan that included unmedicated vaginal birth can be experienced as deeply positive if the couple felt included in the decision. An uncomplicated vaginal birth can be experienced as traumatic if the person felt decisions were made without them.

This is why the doula and midwifery literature consistently teaches one practice above all others for flexibility: If/Then thinking.

Before labor, work through these scenarios together:

If / Then Scenario
If labor is not progressing and augmentation is recommended... Then our preference is to use the BRAIN framework, ask for 15 minutes to discuss, and make the decision together.
If / Then Scenario
If I ask for an epidural even if our plan was unmedicated... Then the partner's job is to support that decision immediately and without question — it is always the birthing person's choice.
If / Then Scenario
If a cesarean becomes necessary... Then our priorities are: skin-to-skin in the OR if possible, partner stays with baby at all times, delayed cord clamping if safe, and we discuss what happened together afterward.
If / Then Scenario
If the birth goes very differently from everything we planned... Then we remember that the preferences document was a communication tool — not a measure of success. A healthy baby and a birthing person who felt heard are the only measures that matter.
```
Part 4 — Communicating Your Preferences

How you say it matters as much as what you say

A national survey of 567 obstetric providers found that 66.5% did not routinely recommend birth plans — and that nearly one-third believed they were predictive of poor outcomes. The research suggests this perception is driven not by birth plans themselves, but by birth plans that are long, demanding, or presented for the first time during active labor.

Here is what actually works:

```
✓ Frame it this way
✓ "We would appreciate delayed cord clamping if possible."
✓ "What is your practice around skin-to-skin after birth?"
✓ "We've prepared a one-page preferences document — can we walk through it together at our 36-week visit?"
✓ "We understand plans change — we're flexible. These are just our starting priorities."
✓ Discuss at 32–36 weeks — not for the first time at admission
✗ Avoid this framing
✗ "You must delay cord clamping."
✗ "Under no circumstances should anyone give my baby formula."
✗ Handing a 4-page document to a nurse during active labor
✗ Treating preferences as non-negotiable demands regardless of circumstances
✗ Never discussing it with your provider before the birth day

The research confirms it: couples who discussed their birth plan with care providers at a prenatal visit — using collaborative, curious language — reported higher satisfaction, greater sense of control, and better preparation for unexpected decisions. The conversation also revealed misalignments early enough to address them — or, if significant, to consider a different provider.

```
