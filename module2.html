Before You Begin

This is the module that changes everything.

Module 1 gave you the map. Module 2 gives you the tools.

Knowing what labor is and being able to support someone through it are two different things. This module is about the second one. The concrete, physical, in-the-moment things you can do — that have been studied, taught, and practiced across hundreds of thousands of births — that make a real difference to the person you love.

You are not a bystander. You are not there to watch. You are there to do something. This module teaches you exactly what that something is.

The most comprehensive review of labor support ever conducted — a Cochrane systematic review pooling data from 27 randomized controlled trials involving nearly 16,000 women across 17 countries — found that continuous, engaged support during labor produces measurable improvements across every major birth outcome. Shorter labors. Fewer cesareans. Less pain medication. Higher satisfaction.

"Continuous support leads to a 25% reduction in cesarean risk overall — and when the support comes from someone in a doula role, the risk reduction increases to 39%. No harms were identified."

Bohren et al. · Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews · 2017

These numbers come from the partner being engaged and present — not just in the room. A 2023 study of 980 births found that only continuous partner presence — not intermittent, not checking in — predicted lower-intervention births. Being present throughout matters. What you do while you're present matters. This module is about both.

The Framework

The 3 R's — your coaching structure

Penny Simkin is a physical therapist, co-founder of DONA International, and the author of The Birth Partner — required reading for DONA certification and arguably the most influential text in birth support education. Over decades of practice and research, she identified a simple but powerful framework for what effective labor support looks like:

```
R
Relaxation
Keeping adrenaline low and endorphins flowing. Slow breathing, a calm environment, your steady presence. When the birthing person is relaxed between contractions, labor progresses more efficiently.
R
Rhythm
Swaying, rocking, circling on a birth ball, walking. Movement helps the baby descend and gives the laboring person something to focus on and anchor to. Match their rhythm — don't try to change it.
R
Ritual
A repetitive activity the laboring person settles into during each contraction — vocalizing, rocking, focused breathing. Your job is to protect and support that ritual, not interrupt it.

The 3 R's are not just theory. Simkin's research connects maintaining relaxation directly to hormone balance: a calm environment keeps adrenaline low and oxytocin (the hormone that drives contractions) flowing. When partners are coached to support relaxation, rhythm, and ritual, they are not just being emotionally supportive — they are actively influencing the physiological progress of labor.

Source: Simkin, P. (2007). Comfort in Labor. Childbirth Connection; Simkin & Bolding (2004). JMWH, 49(6), 489–504.

```
Part 1 — The Techniques

What you actually do

These are the hands-on tools. Learn them before labor. Practice them now. By the time you need them they should feel familiar, not foreign.

```
✋
Counter-Pressure
Most used

What it is: Steady, firm pressure applied to the lower back or sacrum during contractions — particularly valuable during back labor (when the baby is facing the wrong way).

Why it works: Counter-pressure floods the nervous system with competing sensory input, reducing pain perception through the "gate control" mechanism. It also helps relax the surrounding muscles and reduces the pressure the baby's position places on the lower back.

How to do it

Make a fist or use the heel of your hand. Press firmly and steadily into the center of the lower back or the two bony protrusions on either side (the sacrum). Do not rub — press and hold during the contraction. Ask for feedback: more or less pressure, slightly higher or lower. The "Cook Counter-Pressure" technique, documented in Penny Simkin's practice, involves pressing into the ischial tuberosities (sit bones) while the laboring person is on hands and knees — this is one of the most effective positions for back labor relief.

📊 Massage reduced pain scores significantly across latent, active, and transition phases — Ranjbaran et al., 2017
🤝
The Double Hip Squeeze
Partner technique

What it is: The partner stands behind the laboring person and presses inward on both sides of the hips during contractions, compressing the hip joints and opening the pelvic outlet.

Why it works: The hip squeeze temporarily widens the diameter of the pelvic outlet, reducing pressure from the baby's descent and providing significant pain relief. It is one of the most commonly taught and consistently valued techniques in doula practice — documented in Simkin's The Labor Progress Handbook as a core comfort intervention.

How to do it

Stand directly behind the laboring person while they lean forward — hands and knees, leaning over a birth ball, or leaning against a wall or your shoulders. Place both palms flat on the outer surface of the hips, at the widest point. As a contraction begins, press firmly inward and slightly upward — not downward. Hold the pressure throughout the contraction. Release slowly. Ask for feedback on pressure and placement.

📊 Documented in Simkin & Ancheta, The Labor Progress Handbook, 3rd Ed.
💧
Hydrotherapy — Water as Pain Relief
Highly evidence-based

What it is: Labor in water — either a warm shower directed at the lower back, or immersion in a birth tub if available at your birth setting.

Why it works: Warm water relaxes muscles, promotes endorphin release, and reduces the sensation of contractions. A 2024 systematic review of 20 studies involving over 8,000 women found that hydrotherapy significantly reduced pain intensity compared to standard care. The American College of Nurse-Midwives notes that water immersion may reduce the need for pharmacologic labor augmentation by up to 83%.

Partner's role in hydrotherapy

If a tub or shower is available, encourage its use — especially in active labor. If in a shower, direct the spray to the lower back during contractions. Be present — sit outside the tub, maintain eye contact, keep conversation calm. If the laboring person is in a tub, you can use a cup to pour warm water over the abdomen during contractions. Monitor temperature — water should be warm but not hot (37–38°C / 98–100°F). Stay close.

📊 Ergin & Taskin, International Journal of Nursing Practice, 2024 · ACNM Position Statement, 2024
🔄
Position Changes
Every 20–30 min

What it is: Encouraging and supporting the laboring person to change positions regularly throughout labor — not staying in one position for extended periods.

Why it works: Different positions use gravity differently, encourage the baby to rotate into optimal position, relieve pressure from constant bearing on one area, and keep labor progressing. Movement is one of the most evidence-supported non-pharmacological approaches in the doula and midwifery literature.

Positions to know and suggest

Hands and knees: Excellent for back labor — takes pressure off the sacrum and encourages the baby to rotate. Also reduces back pain significantly.

Slow dance: The laboring person drapes their arms around your neck; you hold them and sway together. Deeply comforting — combines movement with physical closeness.

Sitting on a birth ball: Opens the pelvis, allows gentle rocking and swaying, keeps the baby in a favorable position.

Side-lying: Good for rest during a long labor. Use a peanut ball between the knees to keep the pelvis open.

Supported squat: Opens the pelvic outlet significantly during pushing. Stand behind and support under the arms, or use a squat bar if available.

📊 Simkin & Bolding, Journal of Midwifery and Women's Health, 2004
🌬️
Breathing Cues
For every contraction

What it is: Breathing visibly and audibly with the laboring person — modeling slow, steady breath and inviting them to match it.

Why it works: Controlled breathing maintains relaxation, prevents oxygen deprivation, and gives the laboring person a focal point during contractions. Even brief reassuring touch combined with steady breathing — as short as 5–10 seconds — has been shown in randomized trials to reduce expressions of anxiety and lower blood pressure during late active labor.

How to guide breathing

Don't instruct. Model. Breathe in slowly through your nose, out through your mouth — visibly and audibly. If they're holding their breath or tensing, simply breathe and say quietly: "breathe with me." Follow their lead — if they have a rhythm that's working, match it. Don't try to change a ritual that's working. Only intervene if they're clearly hyperventilating or holding their breath entirely.

📊 Simkin, Comfort in Labor, Childbirth Connection, 2007

Part 2 — Your Words

What to say — and what not to say

Words matter in the birth room. The right ones anchor. The wrong ones — even well-intentioned ones — can unravel focus. Penny Simkin's advice from decades of practice: if the laboring person criticizes you or rejects a technique, don't take it personally. She is saying that labor is so difficult right now that nothing helps. You are the safest person for her to express that to. Stay steady.

```
✓ Say this
✓ "I'm right here."
✓ "You're doing it."
✓ "Breathe with me."
✓ "That one's over — you did it."
✓ "You're closer with every contraction."
✓ "You're so strong."
✓ "Tell me what you need."
✓ "I've got you."
✓ Short, simple, repeated. Rhythm matters.
✗ Don't say this
✗ "Calm down." — invalidating, impossible to execute
✗ "Just breathe." — dismissive, not actionable
✗ "How much longer?" — creates anxiety, ask the nurse
✗ "You should try..." — not the moment for suggestions
✗ "You've got this" on repeat — loses meaning fast
✗ Silence + phone — presence is the job
✗ "It's almost over." — if it isn't, you lose trust
✗ "You can do this." — implied doubt; say "you ARE doing it"
```
Part 3 — Why This Matters Long After Birth

Birth memories last a lifetime.

```

"Women recall their birth experiences with remarkable accuracy 15 to 20 years later — and the actions and words of their partners are among the most vividly remembered details."

Penny Simkin · Birth, 18(4), 1991 and Birth, 19(2), 1992

Penny Simkin's landmark 1991 study demonstrated that women remember their births with vivid detail for decades — and that the quality of support they received was a major determinant of whether those memories are positive or negative. Women with the highest long-term satisfaction felt they had accomplished something important, felt in control, and had positive memories of the specific actions and words of their partners and caregivers.

A 2025 randomized controlled trial confirmed this: women who received engaged companion support had significantly higher satisfaction scores across every measured dimension — and partner support during birth has been associated with lower depression and anxiety symptoms even two years after birth.

What you do in that room doesn't just affect the birth. It shapes how both of you remember the day your child was born. It shapes the story you tell. That is not a small thing.

Sources: Güner & Yildirim, BMC Pregnancy and Childbirth, 2025; Evans et al., Birth, 2023
